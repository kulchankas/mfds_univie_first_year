Define a metric $d$ on a set $X\neq\emptyset$, and when $(X,d)$ is a metric space.
$d:X\times X\to[0,\infty)$ is a metric if for all $x,y,z$: (M1) symmetry $d(x,y)=d(y,x)$; (M2) triangle inequality $d(x,z)\le d(x,y)+d(y,z)$; (M3) definiteness $d(x,y)=0\iff x=y$. Then $(X,d)$ is a metric space.
When does a sequence $(x_n)\subseteq X$ converge to $x$ in a metric space?
$x_n\to x$ iff $\forall\varepsilon>0\ \exists N\ \forall n\ge N:\ d(x_n,x)\le\varepsilon$. Equivalently $d(x_n,x)\to 0$.
If $x_n\to x$ and $x_n\to x'$ in a metric space — conclusion, and the one-line proof?
Limits are unique: $x=x'$. Proof: $d(x,x')\le d(x,x_n)+d(x_n,x')\to 0$, so $d(x,x')=0$, hence $x=x'$.
Define order $r>1$, linear, and sublinear convergence of $x_k\to x$.
Order $r$: $\exists c>0:\ d(x_{k+1},x)\le c\,d(x_k,x)^r$.  Linear: $\exists c\in(0,1):\ d(x_{k+1},x)\le c\,d(x_k,x)$.  Sublinear: $\limsup_k d(x_k,x)^{1/k}=1$.
What is an isometry $f:X\to Y$?
$d_Y(f(x),f(x'))=d_X(x,x')$ for all $x,x'$ — it preserves distances.
When is a sequence (or subset) in a metric space bounded?
$\exists x\in X,\ C>0:\ d(x_n,x)\le C$ for all $n$ (resp. $d(s,x)\le C$ for all $s\in S$). The anchor point $x$ is arbitrary — changing it only changes $C$.
Why is every convergent sequence bounded?
Past some $N$, $d(x_n,x)<1$; then $d(x_n,x)\le\max\{d(x_1,x),\dots,d(x_{N-1},x),1\}$ for all $n$.
Give the taxicab, maximum, and discrete metrics.
Taxicab: $d_1(x,y)=|x_1-y_1|+|x_2-y_2|$.  Maximum: $d_{\max}(x,y)=\max\{|x_1-y_1|,|x_2-y_2|\}$.  Discrete: $d(x,y)=0$ if $x=y$, else $1$ (converges $\iff$ eventually constant).
In $\mathbb R$, when does monotonicity force convergence?
A sequence that is monotonically increasing and bounded above converges (to its supremum). Uses order-completeness of $\mathbb R$.
State the density of $\mathbb Q$ in $\mathbb R$.
For every $x\in\mathbb R$ there is $(x_n)\subseteq\mathbb Q$ with $x_n\to x$. We say $\mathbb Q$ is dense in $\mathbb R$.
Define a Cauchy sequence.
$\forall\varepsilon>0\ \exists N\ \forall n,m\ge N:\ d(x_n,x_m)\le\varepsilon$ — terms get arbitrarily close to each other, no limit required.
Relationship between convergent and Cauchy sequences?
Every convergent sequence is Cauchy. (The converse holds only in complete spaces.)
When is a metric space complete?
When every Cauchy sequence converges to a limit inside the space.
Which of $\mathbb Q,\ \mathbb R,\ \mathbb C$ are complete?
$\mathbb R$ and $\mathbb C$ are complete; $\mathbb Q$ is not. Counterexample: the iteration $x_{n+1}=\tfrac12(x_n+2/x_n)$ is Cauchy in $\mathbb Q$ but $\to\sqrt 2\notin\mathbb Q$.
What is a subsequence, and how does it relate to convergence?
$(x_{n_j})_j$ via strictly increasing indices $n_0every subsequence $\to x$.
State Bolzano–Weierstraß.
Every bounded sequence in $\mathbb R$ has a convergent subsequence. (Proof by repeated interval bisection / nested intervals.)
Define (sequential) compactness of a metric space $X$.
Every sequence in $X$ has a subsequence converging to a limit in $X$ — sequences cannot "escape" the space.
Does compact imply complete?
Yes. A Cauchy sequence has a convergent subsequence (compactness), and a Cauchy sequence with a convergent subsequence converges.
Define the ball $B_\varepsilon(x)$ and the open (interior) ball $\mathring B_\varepsilon(x)$.
$B_\varepsilon(x)=\{y\in X: d(x,y)\le\varepsilon\}$ (this text's $B$ is the closed ball); $\mathring B_\varepsilon(x)=\{y: d(x,y)<\varepsilon\}$ (open).
Define open, closed, and neighborhood.
$S$ open: $\forall x\in S\ \exists\varepsilon>0:\ B_\varepsilon(x)\subseteq S$.  $S$ closed: $X\setminus S$ is open.  Neighborhood of $x$: a set containing some ball $B_\varepsilon(x)$. ($\emptyset$ and $X$ are both open and closed.)
Sequential characterization of a closed set $S$?
$S$ is closed $\iff$ it contains the limit of every convergent sequence $(x_n)\subseteq S$ with $x_n\to x\in X$.
What does compactness imply about a subset (any metric space)?
A compact subset is closed and bounded. (Unbounded ⇒ an "escape" sequence; not closed ⇒ a limit outside, both contradict compactness.)
State the Heine–Borel theorem in $\mathbb R$.
$K\subseteq\mathbb R$ (standard metric) is compact $\iff$ closed and bounded. E.g. $[a,b]$ is compact; $(0,1)$ is not.
For $S\subseteq\mathbb R$ bounded above, how does $\sup S$ relate to sequences in $S$?
There is a sequence $(x_n)\subseteq S$ with $x_n\to\sup S$ (dually for $\inf$). So the supremum/infimum of a set are limits of points of the set.
If $a_n\to a$ in $\mathbb R$ and $a_n\ge 0$ for all $n$ — what about $a$?
$a\ge 0$. Convergence respects non-strict inequalities (strict can fail: $1/n>0$ but $\to 0$).
If $a_n\to a$, $b_n\to b$ and $a_n\le b_n$ for all $n$ — conclusion?
$a\le b$. (Apply 1.1.18 to $c_n=b_n-a_n\ge 0$.)
Define the truncated metric $\tilde d=\min\{1,d\}$ and state what it preserves.
$\tilde d(x,y)=\min\{1,d(x,y)\}$ is a metric, and it preserves both convergence and compactness: $x_n\to x$ in $d\iff$ in $\tilde d$, and $K$ compact in $d\iff$ in $\tilde d$. (So every metric is equivalent to a bounded one — boundedness alone is not a metric invariant.)
