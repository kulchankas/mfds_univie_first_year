Day 1 Metric spaces, completeness, compactness covers PS1 · 📖 11–23
🎯 Today
  1. Prove $d(x,y)=|\arctan x-\arctan y|$ is a metric on $\mathbb R$.
    (M1) symmetry and (M2) triangle inequality are inherited from $|\cdot|$ on $\mathbb R$. (M3): $d(x,y)=0\iff\arctan x=\arctan y\iff x=y$, since $\arctan$ is injective. (It is the pullback of the standard metric by the injection $\arctan$.)
  2. Which are compact in $\mathbb R$? (a) $[0,1)$  (b) $\{0\}\cup\{1/n:n\ge1\}$  (c) $\mathbb N$.
    (a) No: $x_n=1-\tfrac1n\to1\notin[0,1)$, so no subsequence converges in the set. (b) Yes: bounded and closed (its only limit point $0$ is included) ⇒ Heine–Borel. (c) No: unbounded.
🌱 Stretch
  1. In a set with the discrete metric, show a subset is compact $\iff$ it is finite.
    Finite: any sequence repeats a value infinitely ⇒ has a constant (convergent) subsequence. Infinite: choose distinct $x_1,x_2,\dots$; pairwise distance is $1$, so no subsequence is Cauchy ⇒ none converges ⇒ not compact.
Day 2 Continuity, preimages, connectedness covers PS2 · 📖 24–38
🔁 Review · Day 1
  1. Use the sequential characterization to show $\{0\}\cup\{1/n\}$ is closed.
    Take $(x_k)$ in the set with $x_k\to x$. Either it is eventually constant ($x$ is in the set), or it has infinitely many distinct $1/n$ terms, forcing $x=0$, which is in the set. It contains all its limits ⇒ closed.
🎯 Today
  1. Show $f(x)=1/x$ is continuous on $(0,\infty)$ using sequences.
    If $x_n\to x>0$, eventually $x_n>x/2$, so $|1/x_n-1/x|=\dfrac{|x-x_n|}{x_nx}\le \dfrac{2}{x^2}|x_n-x|\to0$.
  2. For $f(x)=x^2$ compute $f^{-1}\big((-1,1)\big)$ and confirm it is open.
    $x^2<1\iff x\in(-1,1)$ and $x^2>-1$ always, so $f^{-1}((-1,1))=(-1,1)$ — open, as the continuity theorem predicts for the preimage of an open set.
  3. Is $\{(x,y)\in\mathbb R^2:xy>0\}$ path-connected?
    No — it is quadrants I and III, which are disjoint. A path between a point of I and a point of III would have a connected image and must cross $\{xy\le0\}$. Each quadrant separately is path-connected (convex).
🌱 Stretch
  1. Prove a continuous $f:[0,1]\to\mathbb R$ is bounded and attains a maximum.
    $[0,1]$ is compact ⇒ $f([0,1])$ is compact in $\mathbb R$ ⇒ closed and bounded. Bounded gives boundedness; $\sup f([0,1])$ lies in the (closed) image, so it is attained — the Extreme Value Theorem.
Day 3 Contractions, fixed points, path-connectedness covers PS2 P4 · 📖 24–38
🔁 Review · Days 1–2
  1. Is $\mathbb R^2\setminus\{(0,0)\}$ path-connected?
    Yes. For two points, use the straight segment if it misses the origin; if it passes through $0$, detour via a third point off that line. The punctured plane is path-connected.
🎯 Today
  1. Show $f(x)=\tfrac12\big(x+\tfrac2x\big)$ maps $[1,2]\to[1,2]$, is a contraction, and find its fixed point.
    $f'(x)=\tfrac12(1-2/x^2)$; on $[1,2]$, $2/x^2\in[\tfrac12,2]$ so $f'\in[-\tfrac12,\tfrac14]$, $|f'|\le\tfrac12<1$ ⇒ contraction ($L=\tfrac12$). $f(1)=f(2)=\tfrac32$ and the min is $f(\sqrt2)=\sqrt2$, so $f([1,2])\subseteq[\sqrt2,\tfrac32]\subset[1,2]$. Fixed point: $x=\tfrac12(x+2/x)\Rightarrow x=\sqrt2$ (the Heron iteration).
  2. For $g(x)=\cos x$ on $[0,1]$ ($L=\sin 1\approx0.841$), bound the iterations from $x_0=0$ to reach error $10^{-3}$, using $d(x_n,x^*)\le \tfrac{L^n}{1-L}d(x_1,x_0)$.
    $d(x_1,x_0)=|\cos 0-0|=1$. Need $\tfrac{0.841^{\,n}}{0.159}\le10^{-3}$, i.e. $0.841^{\,n}\le1.59\times10^{-4}$, so $n\ge \dfrac{\ln(1.59\times10^{-4})}{\ln 0.841}\approx\dfrac{-8.75}{-0.173}\approx 51$ steps.
🌱 Stretch
  1. Show completeness is essential: give a contraction with no fixed point.
    $f(x)=x/2$ on $(0,1)$ is a contraction ($L=\tfrac12$), but the only candidate fixed point is $0\notin(0,1)$ — so it has none. The space $(0,1)$ is not complete.
Day 4 Curves & arc length covers PS3 · 📖 135–139
🔁 Review · Days 1–3
  1. Does $f(x)=\tfrac14(x^2+1)$ have a unique fixed point on $[0,1]$? Find it.
    $f'(x)=x/2$, $|f'|\le\tfrac12$ on $[0,1]$; $f([0,1])\subseteq[\tfrac14,\tfrac12]$. Contraction on the complete $[0,1]$ ⇒ unique fixed point, solving $\tfrac14(x^2+1)=x\Rightarrow x^2-4x+1=0\Rightarrow x=2-\sqrt3\approx0.268$.
🎯 Today
  1. Length of $\gamma(t)=(\cos t,\sin t)$, $t\in[0,\pi]$.
    $\|\gamma'(t)\|=\|(-\sin t,\cos t)\|=1$, so length $=\int_0^\pi 1\,dt=\pi$.
  2. Length of $\gamma(t)=\big(t,\tfrac23 t^{3/2}\big)$, $t\in[0,3]$.
    $\gamma'=(1,t^{1/2})$, $\|\gamma'\|=\sqrt{1+t}$. Length $=\int_0^3\sqrt{1+t}\,dt=\big[\tfrac23(1+t)^{3/2}\big]_0^3=\tfrac23(8-1)=\tfrac{14}{3}$.
🌱 Stretch
  1. Show arc length is invariant under an increasing $C^1$ reparametrization $\sigma=\gamma\circ\phi$.
    $\sigma'(s)=\gamma'(\phi(s))\phi'(s)$, $\|\sigma'(s)\|=\|\gamma'(\phi(s))\|\,\phi'(s)$ (as $\phi'>0$). Substituting $t=\phi(s)$, $dt=\phi'(s)\,ds$ turns $\int\|\sigma'\|\,ds$ into $\int\|\gamma'(t)\|\,dt$ — same length.
Day 5 Gradient, partials, affine approximation, Jacobian covers PS4 PS5 · 📖 61–70, 125–150
🔁 Review · Days 1–4
  1. Show $f(x,y)=x^2+y^2$ attains a minimum on the closed disk $\{x^2+y^2\le1\}$.
    The closed disk is compact and $f$ continuous ⇒ minimum attained (at the origin, value $0$).
🎯 Today
  1. For $f(x,y)=e^{xy}$, compute $\nabla f$ and the affine approximation at $(1,0)$; estimate $f(1.1,0.1)$.
    $\nabla f=(ye^{xy},xe^{xy})$; at $(1,0)$: $f=1$, $\nabla f=(0,1)$. So $F(x,y)=1+(y-0)=1+y$ and $f(1.1,0.1)\approx1.1$ (true $e^{0.11}\approx1.116$).
  2. For $f(x,y)=(x+y,\,xy)$, find the Jacobian at $(2,3)$ and estimate $f(2.05,2.98)$.
    $J=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\ y&x\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\3&2\end{pmatrix}$; $f(2,3)=(5,6)$, $\Delta=(0.05,-0.02)$. $F=(5,6)+J\Delta=(5+0.03,\,6+0.11)=(5.03,\,6.11)$.
🌱 Stretch
  1. Show $f(x,y)=\tfrac{xy}{x^2+y^2}$ (with $f(0,0)=0$) has both partials at $0$ but is not continuous there.
    On the axes $f\equiv0$, so $f_x(0,0)=f_y(0,0)=0$. But along $y=x$, $f=\tfrac{x^2}{2x^2}=\tfrac12\ne0=f(0,0)$, so $f$ is not continuous at $0$. (Existence of partials ≠ differentiability.)
Day 6 Taylor in $\mathbb R^n$, Hessian, classifying extrema covers PS6 PS7 P1 · 📖 151–158
🔁 Review · Days 1–5
  1. Use the Jacobian of $f(x,y)=(x^2,xy)$ at $(1,1)$ to estimate $f(1.02,0.97)$.
    $J=\begin{pmatrix}2x&0\\ y&x\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}2&0\\1&1\end{pmatrix}$; $f(1,1)=(1,1)$, $\Delta=(0.02,-0.03)$. $F=(1+0.04,\,1+(0.02-0.03))=(1.04,\,0.99)$.
🎯 Today
  1. Find and classify the critical points of $f(x,y)=x^3-3x+y^2$.
    $\nabla f=(3x^2-3,\,2y)=0\Rightarrow x=\pm1,\ y=0$. $H=\begin{pmatrix}6x&0\\0&2\end{pmatrix}$. At $(1,0)$: $\det=12>0,\ 6>0$ ⇒ local min. At $(-1,0)$: $\det=-12<0$ ⇒ saddle.
  2. Classify the critical point of $f(x,y)=x^2+xy+y^2$.
    $\nabla f=(2x+y,\,x+2y)=0\Rightarrow(0,0)$. $H=\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{pmatrix}$, $\det=3>0$, $2>0$ ⇒ (global) minimum.
🌱 Stretch
  1. Second-order Taylor of $f(x,y)=e^{x}\cos y$ at $(0,0)$.
    $f(0,0)=1$, $\nabla f=(e^x\cos y,-e^x\sin y)=(1,0)$, $H=\begin{pmatrix}e^x\cos y&-e^x\sin y\\-e^x\sin y&-e^x\cos y\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}$. So $f\approx 1+x+\tfrac12(x^2-y^2)$.
Day 8 Convexity covers PS7 P3 · 📖 76–78, 129–134
🔁 Review · Days 1–6
  1. Classify the critical point of $f(x,y)=x^2-y^2$.
    $\nabla f=(2x,-2y)=0\Rightarrow(0,0)$; $H=\mathrm{diag}(2,-2)$, $\det=-4<0$ ⇒ saddle.
🎯 Today
  1. Show $f(x)=x^4$ is convex on $\mathbb R$ but not strongly convex near $0$.
    $f''(x)=12x^2\ge0$ ⇒ convex. But $f''(0)=0$, so there is no $m>0$ with $f''\ge m$ on a neighbourhood of $0$ ⇒ not strongly convex there.
  2. Show $\max(f,g)$ of convex $f,g$ is convex; show $\min$ need not be.
    $h=\max(f,g)$: $h(\lambda x+(1-\lambda)y)\le\max(\lambda f(x)+(1-\lambda)f(y),\,\lambda g(x)+(1-\lambda)g(y))\le\lambda h(x)+(1-\lambda)h(y)$. Counterexample for min: $\min(x,-x)=-|x|$ is concave.
🌱 Stretch
  1. Prove AM–GM for two positives via convexity: $\sqrt{ab}\le\tfrac{a+b}{2}$.
    $-\ln$ is convex, so $-\ln\tfrac{a+b}{2}\le\tfrac12(-\ln a)+\tfrac12(-\ln b)=-\ln\sqrt{ab}$. Exponentiating gives $\tfrac{a+b}{2}\ge\sqrt{ab}$.
Day 9 Gradient descent, SGD, $L$-smoothness covers PS7 P4 PS8 · 📖 158–165
🔁 Review · Days 1–8
  1. Is $f(x,y)=x^2+3y^2$ convex? Find the global minimum.
    $H=\mathrm{diag}(2,6)\succ0$ ⇒ strictly convex; minimum at $(0,0)$, value $0$.
🎯 Today
  1. For $f(x)=\tfrac12 x^2$ (so $f'(x)=x$, $L=1$), gradient descent is $x_{k+1}=(1-\eta)x_k$. For which $\eta$ does it converge, and what is the best step?
    Converges $\iff|1-\eta|<1\iff 0<\eta<2$ (note $2/L=2$). Best step $\eta=1$: reaches the minimum in one step.
  2. For $f(x)=\tfrac12 x^\top A x$ with $A=\mathrm{diag}(1,10)$, what step size guarantees convergence, and what sets the rate?
    Need $\eta<2/L=2/10=0.2$. The rate is set by the condition number $\kappa=L/\mu=10/1=10$; the worst-case contraction factor is $\tfrac{\kappa-1}{\kappa+1}=\tfrac{9}{11}$.
🌱 Stretch
  1. Why does SGD use an unbiased gradient estimate, and what is the trade-off versus full-batch GD?
    A random mini-batch gradient $\nabla f_i$ satisfies $\mathbb E[\nabla f_i]=\nabla f$, so steps are correct in expectation. It is far cheaper per step but noisy, which is why it typically needs decreasing step sizes (or averaging); the noise can also help escape saddle points and sharp minima.
Day 10 Newton in 1-D and $\mathbb R^n$, convergence orders covers PS8 P3–4 PS10 · 📖 166–172
🔁 Review · Days 1–9
  1. For $f(x)=\tfrac12 x^2$, compare gradient descent ($\eta=0.5$) with Newton from $x_0=1$.
    GD: $x_{k+1}=0.5x_k$ ⇒ linear, $x_k=2^{-k}$. Newton: $x_{k+1}=x_k-f'/f''=x_k-x_k=0$ — exact in one step (a quadratic objective is solved in a single Newton step).
🎯 Today
  1. Newton for $\sqrt2$: $f(x)=x^2-2$, iterate twice from $x_0=2$.
    $x_{k+1}=x_k-\tfrac{x_k^2-2}{2x_k}=\tfrac12\big(x_k+\tfrac2{x_k}\big)$. $x_1=\tfrac12(2+1)=1.5$; $x_2=\tfrac12(1.5+1.3333\ldots)=1.41\overline6$, already $\approx\sqrt2=1.41421\ldots$.
  2. State Newton's order of convergence near a simple root and why.
    Quadratic ($r=2$): $e_{k+1}\approx\tfrac{f''(x^*)}{2f'(x^*)}e_k^2$, from Taylor-expanding $f$ about the root. It needs $f'(x^*)\ne0$ (simple root) and $x_0$ close enough.
🌱 Stretch
  1. Build sequences with linear vs quadratic convergence to $0$ and compare.
    Linear: $a_k=2^{-k}$ ($a_{k+1}/a_k=\tfrac12$). Quadratic: $b_k=2^{-2^{k}}$ ($b_{k+1}/b_k^{2}=1$). After a few steps $b_k$ is astronomically smaller — quadratic roughly doubles the number of correct digits each step.
Day 11 Newton edge cases · implicit / inverse function theorem covers PS10 · 📖 166–172
🔁 Review · Days 1–10
  1. What does Newton do on $f(x)=x^3-2x+2$ from $x_0=0$?
    $f'=3x^2-2$. $x_1=0-\tfrac{2}{-2}=1$; $f(1)=1$, $f'(1)=1$, $x_2=1-\tfrac11=0$. It enters the $2$-cycle $0,1,0,1,\dots$ — Newton can cycle or diverge when $x_0$ is poor or $f'$ is small.
🎯 Today
  1. By the IFT, where does $x^2+y^2=1$ define $y=g(x)$? Compute $g'$.
    $F=x^2+y^2-1$, $F_y=2y\ne0\iff y\ne0$. There $g'(x)=-F_x/F_y=-x/y$.
  2. For $F(x,y)=xe^{y}+y=0$ near $(0,0)$, find $g'(0)$ with $y=g(x)$.
    $F(0,0)=0$; $F_y=xe^y+1=1\ne0$ at $(0,0)$; $F_x=e^y=1$. So $g'(0)=-F_x/F_y=-1$.
🌱 Stretch
  1. Is $f(x,y)=(e^x\cos y,\,e^x\sin y)$ locally invertible at $(0,0)$?
    $J=\begin{pmatrix}e^x\cos y&-e^x\sin y\\ e^x\sin y&e^x\cos y\end{pmatrix}$, $\det=e^{2x}=1\ne0$ at $(0,0)$ ⇒ by the Inverse Function Theorem it is locally invertible (this is the complex exponential).
Day 12 Implicit/Inverse FT · submanifolds · tangent space covers PS11 · 📖 166–179
🔁 Review · Days 1–11
  1. Does the folium $x^3+y^3=6xy$ define $y=g(x)$ near $(3,3)$? Find $g'(3)$.
    $F=x^3+y^3-6xy$, $F_y=3y^2-6x=27-18=9\ne0$ ⇒ yes. $F_x=3x^2-6y=27-18=9$, so $g'(3)=-F_x/F_y=-1$.
🎯 Today
  1. Show the unit sphere $S=\{x^2+y^2+z^2=1\}$ is a $2$-submanifold and find the tangent plane at $(0,0,1)$.
    $g=x^2+y^2+z^2-1$, $\nabla g=(2x,2y,2z)\ne0$ on $S$ ⇒ regular value ⇒ $2$-manifold. The tangent space is $\ker dg=\{v:\nabla g\cdot v=0\}$; at $(0,0,1)$, $\nabla g=(0,0,2)$, so the tangent plane is $z=1$ (directions with $v_3=0$, the horizontal plane).
  2. Tangent line to $\{x^2+y^2=1\}$ at $\big(\tfrac{\sqrt2}{2},\tfrac{\sqrt2}{2}\big)$.
    $\nabla g=(2x,2y)\propto(1,1)$, so the tangent direction is $(1,-1)$ and the line is $x+y=\sqrt2$.
🌱 Stretch
  1. Why is the regularity condition $\nabla g\ne0$ needed? Give a set where it fails.
    $\{y^2=x^3\}$ (a cusp): with $g=y^2-x^3$, $\nabla g=(-3x^2,2y)=(0,0)$ at the origin. The level set has a singular cusp there and is not a smooth manifold.
Day 13 Lagrange multipliers · constrained extrema covers PS12 PS13 PS14 P1 · 📖 173–183
🔁 Review · Days 1–12
  1. Tangent plane to the graph $z=x^2+y^2$ at $(1,1,2)$.
    As a level set $g=z-x^2-y^2$, $\nabla g=(-2x,-2y,1)=(-2,-2,1)$. Tangent plane: $-2(x-1)-2(y-1)+(z-2)=0\Rightarrow z=2x+2y-2$.
🎯 Today
  1. Extremize $f(x,y)=x+y$ on $x^2+y^2=1$.
    $\nabla f=\lambda\nabla g$: $(1,1)=\lambda(2x,2y)\Rightarrow x=y=\tfrac1{2\lambda}$; the constraint gives $x=y=\pm\tfrac1{\sqrt2}$. Max $=\sqrt2$ at $(\tfrac1{\sqrt2},\tfrac1{\sqrt2})$, min $=-\sqrt2$.
  2. Minimize $f(x,y)=x^2+y^2$ subject to $x+2y=5$.
    $(2x,2y)=\lambda(1,2)\Rightarrow x=\tfrac\lambda2,\ y=\lambda$; constraint $\tfrac\lambda2+2\lambda=5\Rightarrow\lambda=2$, so $(x,y)=(1,2)$ and the minimum is $1+4=5$.
🌱 Stretch
  1. Prove AM–GM for three positives via Lagrange: maximize $xyz$ with $x+y+z=s$ fixed.
    $(yz,xz,xy)=\lambda(1,1,1)\Rightarrow xy=yz=zx\Rightarrow x=y=z=s/3$. So $\max xyz=(s/3)^3$, giving $\sqrt[3]{xyz}\le\tfrac{x+y+z}{3}$.
Day 14 Operator norms · (von) Neumann series covers PS9 PS14 P3 · 📖 101–111
🔁 Review · Days 1–13
  1. Find the closest point of the plane $x+y+z=1$ to the origin (via Lagrange) and the distance.
    Minimize $x^2+y^2+z^2$ on the plane: $(2x,2y,2z)=\lambda(1,1,1)\Rightarrow x=y=z$; constraint ⇒ $x=y=z=\tfrac13$. Distance$^2=3\cdot\tfrac19=\tfrac13$, distance $=\tfrac1{\sqrt3}$.
🎯 Today
  1. Compute $\|A\|_1$ and $\|A\|_\infty$ for $A=\begin{pmatrix}1&-2\\3&4\end{pmatrix}$.
    $\|A\|_1=$ max absolute column sum $=\max(1+3,\,2+4)=6$. $\|A\|_\infty=$ max absolute row sum $=\max(1+2,\,3+4)=7$.
  2. For $A=\begin{pmatrix}0&1/2\\ 1/3&0\end{pmatrix}$ compute $(I-A)^{-1}$ and relate it to the Neumann series.
    $I-A=\begin{pmatrix}1&-1/2\\-1/3&1\end{pmatrix}$, $\det=1-\tfrac16=\tfrac56$, so $(I-A)^{-1}=\tfrac{6}{5}\begin{pmatrix}1&1/2\\1/3&1\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}6/5&3/5\\2/5&6/5\end{pmatrix}$. Since $\|A\|_\infty=\tfrac12<1$, this equals $\sum_{k\ge0}A^k$.
🌱 Stretch
  1. Show: if $\|A\|<1$ in some operator norm, then $I-A$ is invertible with $\|(I-A)^{-1}\|\le\tfrac{1}{1-\|A\|}$.
    The Neumann series $\sum_{k\ge0}A^k$ converges absolutely because $\sum\|A\|^k=\tfrac1{1-\|A\|}<\infty$. Its sum $S$ satisfies $(I-A)S=S(I-A)=I$, so $S=(I-A)^{-1}$, and $\|S\|\le\sum\|A\|^k=\tfrac1{1-\|A\|}$.
