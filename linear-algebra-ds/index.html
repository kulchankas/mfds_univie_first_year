🤖 In data science
🧠 ML intuition
Goal

Build the vocabulary for where data lives: vector spaces and subspaces, linear maps between spaces, dual vectors (linear functionals), and norms that measure size and stability. This chapter answers: what structure do we assume when we write $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ or $f: V \to W$?

Why ML/DS needs it

Every feature vector is a point in a space; every weight matrix is a linear map. Embeddings map discrete objects into continuous spaces. Regularization (L1/L2) is choosing a norm. The operator norm $\|A\|$ bounds how much a perturbation in input can blow up in output — central to conditioning, adversarial robustness, and understanding why some layers amplify noise.

Keep in mind

$\mathbb{R}^n$ is the workhorse, but the axioms matter: not every subset is a subspace (e.g. the first quadrant fails closure under negation). The dual space $V^*$ previews gradients and inner products in Ch 2. A matrix depends on your choice of basis; the underlying linear map does not. Operator norm is not the largest entry of the matrix.

Intuition to develop

Picture your dataset as a cloud of points in a high-dimensional room. A linear layer pushes that cloud through a fixed "door" (linear map) — no bending, only stretch, rotate, and shear. A norm tells you how far a point is from the origin; the operator norm tells you the worst-case stretch the door can apply. Explain to a friend: "We're learning the rules of the room our data lives in, before we measure angles or distances."

🗣 Before moving on
Can you explain, without notes: (1) why $\mathbb{R}^2$ is a vector space but the first quadrant is not? (2) What is the dual of a vector space and why does it matter for inner products later? (3) How does a linear map become a matrix once you pick bases?

🤖 In data science
🧠 ML intuition
Goal

Add geometry to vector spaces: inner products give angles and lengths, orthogonal projection finds the closest point in a subspace, and the adjoint pairs a map with its "reverse" action under the inner product. Least squares — the workhorse of supervised learning — is a projection problem.

Why ML/DS needs it

Linear regression with more features than perfect fit uses least squares: find $\hat{\mathbf{y}} = A\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ closest to $\mathbf{b}$ in the column space of $A$. Gram–Schmidt builds orthonormal bases used in QR decomposition (stable least squares). The adjoint/transpose appears in backpropagation and normal equations $A^*A\hat{x} = A^*b$. Riesz representation links linear functionals to vectors — the seed of kernel methods.

Keep in mind

Projection onto $\mathrm{Col}(A)$ is always defined; the formula $A(A^*A)^{-1}A^*$ needs full column rank. Gram–Schmidt in raw form can be numerically unstable — QR via Householder is what libraries use. Over $\mathbb{C}$, adjoint uses conjugate transpose, not plain transpose. Least squares minimizes $\|Ax - b\|_2$, not entrywise error.

Intuition to develop

Inner product = similarity score between two vectors. Orthogonal = genuinely independent directions. Projection = drop the component you can't explain, keep the best fit inside the model's span. Least squares = "find the compromise hyperplane that gets as close as possible to all target points." Explain to a friend: "We're finally measuring how far off our predictions are, and geometry tells us the unique best fix."

🗣 Before moving on
Can you explain: (1) why least-squares is "project $b$ onto $\mathrm{Col}(A)$"? (2) What Gram–Schmidt actually produces and why orthonormal bases simplify coordinates. (3) What the adjoint of an operator is and how it relates to the transpose.

🎯 Course exercises
🤖 In data science
🧠 ML intuition
Goal

Understand functions of two vectors that are linear in each argument separately — bilinear and sesquilinear forms — and the tensor product $V \otimes W$ as the universal way to combine vector spaces without committing to coordinates first.

Why ML/DS needs it

A symmetric positive-definite bilinear form defines an ellipsoid — the geometry of Mahalanobis distance and Gaussian covariances. Quadratic forms $\mathbf{x}^T Q \mathbf{x}$ appear in loss landscapes and convex optimization (Hessians). Sesquilinear forms over $\mathbb{C}$ underpin complex inner products in quantum ML and signal processing. Tensor products formalize multi-index features, outer products, and higher-order structure in neural tensor networks and kernel design.

Keep in mind

Bilinear = linear in each slot over $\mathbb{R}$; sesquilinear = conjugate-linear in one slot (needed for $\mathbb{C}$). An inner product is a positive-definite sesquilinear form. $V \otimes W$ is not the direct product $V \times W$ — its dimension is $\dim V \cdot \dim W$. Symmetry and positive-definiteness are separate properties; check both.

Intuition to develop

A form is a machine that eats two vectors and outputs one number — like a generalized dot product. A quadratic form eats one vector and asks "how curved is the energy bowl at this point?" The tensor product says: "build a space whose coordinates are all products $v_i \otimes w_j$ — the natural home for interactions between two feature sets." Explain to a friend: "When two inputs interact multiplicatively, you're living in tensor land."

🗣 Before moving on
Can you explain: (1) the difference between bilinear and sesquilinear — and which one matches an inner product over $\mathbb{C}$. (2) Why a symmetric positive-definite form defines an ellipsoid. (3) What $V \otimes W$ buys you that direct product doesn't.

🤖 In data science
🧠 ML intuition
Goal

Define the determinant as the unique alternating $n$-linear form that measures oriented volume, connect it to invertibility, and use it for change-of-variables in multivariate calculus.

Why ML/DS needs it

The Jacobian determinant appears in normalizing flows, VAEs, and any density transformation: $p(\mathbf{z}) = p(\mathbf{x})\,|\det J|^{-1}$. For Gaussians, $\det(\Sigma)$ sets the normalization constant and volume of confidence ellipsoids — hence log-determinant tricks in GP inference and variational methods. Volume scaling explains why invertible layers must preserve (or track) how much probability mass is squeezed or expanded.

Keep in mind

$\det A = 0$ means the map collapses dimension — not invertible. Sign encodes orientation flip. $\det(AB) = \det A \det B$ matches composition. Cramer's rule is pedagogically nice but $O(n!)$ — never used at scale; LU/QR wins. In code, use logdet via Cholesky for positive-definite matrices to avoid overflow.

Intuition to develop

Apply a linear map to the unit cube; the determinant is how much the volume of that cube changes (negative = reflection). For a small nonlinear map, the Jacobian determinant is the local volume stretch. Explain to a friend: "Determinant tells you whether the transformation is reversible and how much space gets squashed or blown up — critical whenever you transform probability densities."

🗣 Before moving on
Can you explain: (1) determinant as volume scaling (sign included). (2) Why $\det(AB)=\det A \det B$ matches composition of linear maps. (3) When Cramer's rule is useful vs. when row reduction wins.

🤖 In data science
🧠 ML intuition
Goal

Find directions that a linear map leaves invariant (eigenvectors) and the corresponding stretch factors (eigenvalues). Diagonalize when possible; otherwise use triangularization over $\mathbb{C}$. Extend to matrix functions like $e^{tA}$ via the characteristic polynomial and Cayley–Hamilton.

Why ML/DS needs it

PCA is eigendecomposition of the covariance matrix — variance along each eigenvector. Spectral clustering and PageRank are eigenvector problems on graph Laplacians/adjacency matrices. Power iteration finds dominant eigenvalues (used in recommender systems and NLP). Matrix exponentials $e^{tA}$ govern linear ODEs and continuous-time Markov chains; they appear in Neural ODEs and stability analysis of recurrent nets.

Keep in mind

Not every matrix is diagonalizable over $\mathbb{R}$ (rotation in $\mathbb{R}^2$ needs $\mathbb{C}$). Algebraic multiplicity $\geq$ geometric multiplicity. Symmetric matrices are the sweet case: real eigenvalues, orthogonal eigenvectors. Cayley–Hamilton says $p(A) = 0$ — useful for defining $f(A)$ but don't confuse characteristic polynomial with minimal polynomial. Large matrices: never compute characteristic polynomial explicitly.

Intuition to develop

An eigenvector is a direction the transformation doesn't bend — only stretches or flips. An eigenvalue tells you the stretch factor. Diagonalization = "find a coordinate system where the map is just scaling along each axis." Explain to a friend: "Eigenstuff finds the natural axes hidden in your data or dynamics — PCA literally ranks them by how much variance they carry."

🗣 Before moving on
Can you explain: (1) geometric meaning of an eigenvector. (2) Why symmetric matrices have real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors. (3) What Cayley–Hamilton says and one place matrix functions use it.

🔬 SVD / PCA mini-lab
  1. By hand ($2\times2$): For $A=\begin{pmatrix}3&1\\1&3\end{pmatrix}$, find singular values and an orthonormal pair of singular vectors. Check with numpy.linalg.svd.
  2. Rank-1 approximation: Take a small grayscale image matrix (e.g. 32×32). Plot original vs. rank-$k$ SVD reconstruction for $k=1,5,20$. Where does most energy live?
  3. PCA pipeline: On a toy dataset (Iris or synthetic 2D clusters): center data → covariance → eigenvectors → project to 2D. Compare to sklearn.decomposition.PCA.
  4. Pseudoinverse: Solve an overdetermined $Ax=b$ via $A^+$ from SVD; match numpy.linalg.lstsq.
  5. Spectral vs SVD: For a symmetric $B$, verify SVD agrees with orthogonal eigendecomposition. When do they differ (non-square $A$)?
  6. Explain Eckart–Young: In one paragraph, why truncating singular values gives the best low-rank approximation in Frobenius norm.
🤖 In data science
🧠 ML intuition
Goal

Diagonalize normal operators (spectral theorem) and decompose any matrix via SVD: $A = U\Sigma V^*$. Understand why these give optimal low-rank approximations and stable solutions to ill-posed linear systems.

Why ML/DS needs it

Truncated SVD = PCA on centered data, dimensionality reduction, denoising, and latent semantic analysis in NLP. Eckart–Young: keep top-$k$ singular values for best rank-$k$ approximation in Frobenius norm — the math behind image compression and recommender systems. Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse via SVD solves least squares even when $A$ is rank-deficient. Spectral theorem guarantees real orthonormal eigenbases for symmetric/Hermitian matrices — the foundation of covariance analysis.

Keep in mind

Spectral theorem requires normality ($AA^* = A^*A$); symmetry is the important special case. SVD always exists for any matrix, even non-square or non-diagonalizable. Singular values of $A$ are $\sqrt{\lambda_i(A^*A)}$ — not the same as eigenvalues of $A$ when $A$ is rectangular. PCA: center first, then eigendecompose covariance (or SVD the centered data matrix). Truncating too aggressively loses signal; too little retains noise.

Intuition to develop

SVD finds three moves: rotate input ($V^*$), stretch along orthogonal axes ($\Sigma$), rotate output ($U$). Every matrix, no matter how messy, has this geometry. Truncating $\Sigma$ keeps the "loud" directions and drops the whispers — that's denoising and compression. Explain to a friend: "SVD is the Swiss Army knife: PCA, recommendations, pseudoinverse, and low-rank approximation all come from the same decomposition."

🗣 Before moving on
Can you explain: (1) why every matrix has an SVD even when it's not diagonalizable. (2) How singular values relate to $\|A\|_2$ and to PCA. (3) What "normal operator" means and why the spectral theorem needs it.
